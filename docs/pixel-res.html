Digital Resolution

Digital resolution tells you how much image data you have – it's the pixel dimensions of your image file (width × height).

A pixel (picture element) is the smallest unit of digital image data. Each pixel stores specific colour information using red, green, and blue values.

Examples:

  • 1920 × 1080 = 2,073,600 pixels total
  • 3000 × 2000 = 6,000,000 pixels total
  • 4000 × 3000 = 12,000,000 pixels total

More pixels = more image data = potential for higher quality output.

Pixels per inch (PPI)

PPI measures how many pixels can fit in a 1-inch line. It's used in two ways:

Physical devices:

Describes pixel density on screens. A modern phone might have 460 PPI (sharp for close viewing), while a billboard might have 10 PPI (adequate for distant viewing).

Software metadata:

In programs like Photoshop, PPI tells other software the intended pixel density when the image is used elsewhere.

Important: Changing PPI in software doesn't change your pixel count – it's metadata that controls physical print size while maintaining quality.

Common PPI Standards

72 PPI

Traditional screen resolution, adequate for web graphics and digital display

150 PPI

High-quality screen display, upper limit of what's perceptible on most monitors

300 PPI

Industry standard for professional printing, ensures smooth reproduction of detail

Retina Displays

Apple devices often use 220+ PPI for screens viewed at typical distances
