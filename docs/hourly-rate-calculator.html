This is what you actually want to see in your bank account each year, after tax. It's not what you invoice — that number (gross income) will be higher once tax, super, and expenses are added. For reference, a graduate designer in Melbourne might aim for $45–55k to start; a midweight designer $70–90k.
Superannuation is compulsory retirement savings. As a freelancer, no one pays it for you — you have to build it into your rate and set it aside yourself. The current legislated rate is 12% of your gross income. If you don't include it, you're effectively working for less than your headline rate every year and arriving at retirement with nothing saved.

As a freelancer you don't get paid leave — every week you're not working is a week you're not earning. This setting reduces your available working hours accordingly, which pushes your hourly rate up to compensate. Standard (4 weeks) is the minimum most people need. Balanced (6 weeks) is realistic once you factor in public holidays and the odd sick day. Don't underestimate this — it's one of the most common mistakes new freelancers make when setting their rates.

Full time — fixed costs spread across the most hours, so rate is lowest
Working part time doesn't reduce your fixed costs — software, insurance, and equipment cost the same whether you work 2 days or 5. So your hourly rate needs to be higher to cover those costs across fewer hours. Your income target also scales down proportionally — 3 days a week means roughly 60% of a full-time salary. The rate stays roughly the same because both income and hours reduce together, but your overheads push it up slightly.
Not every hour at your desk earns money. Replying to emails, writing quotes, chasing invoices, updating your portfolio, and keeping your skills current all take real time. For a freelancer starting out, 20–25% is realistic. If you're also studying or working on personal projects, it could be higher. Underestimating this is a fast way to underprice your work.

Where you work affects your costs. A home office is cheapest but you need a dedicated space — the ATO allows you to claim a portion of rent and utilities. A coworking space gives you a professional environment, fast internet, and separation between work and home life. Rates shown are based on T.O.M.S Place in Brunswick; prices vary by location.

You can't expense a $3,000 laptop all in one year, but you can spread the cost. Divide the purchase price by how many years you expect to use it. For example: a $3,000 computer used for 3 years = $1,000/yr. Include other equipment too — external drives, monitors, drawing tablets, cameras. The ATO calls this depreciation.

Anything you spend getting work. This includes your portfolio website hosting and domain name (typically $100–200/yr), any paid advertising, or printed self-promotion materials. As you become more established this number grows — some designers spend significantly more on building their profile.
$
Professional indemnity insurance covers you if a client claims your work caused them financial harm — for example, a typo in a printed run or a missed deadline that cost them money. It's typically $500–1,500/yr depending on your turnover. Most studios require contractors to hold it before they'll engage you. Set to $0 for now as a student, but factor it in once you start taking commercial work.
$
A basic tax return lodged by an accountant costs $150–400/yr for a sole trader. Once you're earning regularly, it's worth it — an accountant will find deductions you'd miss, keep you on the right side of the ATO, and give you advice on whether to register for GST (required once you earn over $75,000/yr). Set to $0 for now, but include it once you start freelancing properly.

A standard home NBN plan runs about $70–100/month ($840–1,200/yr). If you work from a coworking space that includes internet, set this to $0 — but you may still want to include a portion for home use outside work hours. Mobile data costs can also be included here.

Divide your handset purchase price by how many years you'll use it (e.g. $1,200 phone ÷ 3 years = $400/yr), then add your annual plan cost (e.g. $60/month = $720/yr). Total: $1,120/yr. You can only claim the business-use portion, but for a freelancer that's typically most of it.
